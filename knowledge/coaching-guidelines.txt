FitbodGPT Coaching Guidelines

Comprehensive reference for user assessment, injury handling, recovery interpretation, programming targets, and plan generation. This file is the primary coaching knowledge base.

---

USER LEVEL DETECTION

Score 0-10 on each axis from working sets only, then compute weighted total.

Axes and weights:

Exercise variety (15%):
  <6 unique strength exercises = 0
  6-15 unique = 5
  >15 unique = 10
  Count only strength exercises, not mobility or cardio.

Training frequency (15%):
  <2 sessions/week = 0
  2-4 sessions/week = 5
  4+ sessions/week = 10
  Use overall average across the entire date range.

Compound lift count (15%):
  0-1 distinct compound exercises = 0
  2-4 = 5
  5+ = 10
  Compounds: multi-joint movements (squat, deadlift, bench, row, press, lunge, pull-up, dip, hip thrust).

Overload evidence (20%):
  Among compound exercises that have a non-N/A trend value, calculate the percentage with a positive trend (>0%).
  Map that percentage to 0-10 (e.g., 50% positive = 5.0).
  If no compounds have trend data, score 0.

Volume per session (15%):
  Total working sets across all exercises / total sessions.
  <8 avg working sets/session = 0
  8-16 = 5
  16+ = 10

Data depth (10%):
  <4 weeks of data = 0
  4-26 weeks = 5
  26+ weeks = 10

Exercise sophistication (10%):
  Percentage of total working sets that use free weight equipment (barbell, dumbbell, kettlebell, EZ bar) or are unilateral exercises.
  Map to 0-10 (e.g., 44% = 4.4).

Final score = sum of (axis_score x weight) x 10. Range 0-100.
  0-33: Beginner
  34-66: Intermediate
  67-100: Advanced

Always show the detected level and numeric score. The user can override.
When unsure, default DOWN one level to be safe.

DATA CONFIDENCE TIERS

Based on the weeks covered by the report's date range:

< 4 weeks: LIMITED
  "Limited data - recommendations are preliminary. I can see what exercises you do, but can't reliably detect trends."
  Skip trend analysis. Focus on snapshot of current exercises, equipment, and basic volume assessment.

4-12 weeks: MODERATE
  "Moderate data - solid patterns emerging. Progression trends becoming visible."
  Can identify exercise patterns and volume distribution. Trend data exists but may be sparse for exercises done infrequently.

12+ weeks: STRONG
  "Strong data - high confidence in pattern analysis, progression trends, and imbalance detection."
  Full analysis available. Trends are reliable for exercises appearing in multiple periods.

Additional confidence notes:
- If many exercise trends show N/A, mention that trend analysis is limited even with a long date range.
- If cardio_min is 0 but recent_sessions show running/walking, note the cardio field may undercount actual cardio activity.
- If period_count is much less than weeks (gaps in data), note inconsistent training frequency.

INJURIES AND LIMITATIONS

When the user reports injuries or limitations:

Avoidance:
  Remove or substitute exercises that load the affected area. Always state which exercises were swapped and why. Do not silently remove an exercise the user regularly does without explaining.

Alternatives by injury type:
  Shoulder impingement: Replace overhead press with landmine press, high incline dumbbell press, or cable lateral raise. Replace behind-neck pull with lat pulldown to front.
  Lower back pain: Replace conventional deadlift with trap bar deadlift or hip thrust. Replace back squat with leg press or goblet squat. Avoid heavy bent-over rows; use chest-supported rows.
  Knee pain: Replace deep squats with box squats to parallel or leg press with limited ROM. Focus on hip hinge movements. Avoid heavy leg extensions through full ROM.
  Wrist pain: Use neutral grip dumbbells. Replace straight bar curls with EZ bar or hammer curls. Use wrist wraps.
  Elbow/tennis elbow: Reduce or eliminate heavy curls and tricep extensions. Use neutral grip. Reduce training frequency for arms.

Rehab mode:
  If the user wants rehab-focused programming, shift the plan to prioritize:
  - Controlled range of motion exercises for the affected area
  - Light loading with gradual progression (smaller increments than normal)
  - Mobility work before strength work for the affected area
  - Higher rep ranges (12-20) with lighter loads for rebuilding
  - Include exercises that promote blood flow and healing
  Do not replace physiotherapy. This supplements professional treatment.

Risk flagging:
  If an exercise in the user's history could aggravate a reported injury, mention it proactively even if the user did not ask. For example, if they report shoulder pain but their history shows overhead pressing, flag it.

Serious injuries:
  For acute injuries, post-surgical recovery, or persistent pain during exercise, recommend consulting a physiotherapist or sports medicine professional before training the affected area. You can suggest supporting exercises for unaffected areas, but do not program the injured area without explicit clearance.

RECOVERY DATA

Users may optionally share recovery metrics alongside their training report.

Accepted metrics:
  - Sleep hours per night
  - Resting heart rate (RHR) - beats per minute
  - Heart rate variability (HRV) - milliseconds
  - Subjective energy levels (1-10 or descriptive)
  - Soreness ratings (1-10 or by body area)
  - Stress level (1-10 or descriptive)

How to interpret and apply:

Poor recovery signals:
  Sleep <6 hours, RHR elevated above personal baseline, HRV significantly lower than normal, energy 1-4/10, high soreness across multiple areas.
  Action: Reduce session intensity. Suggest lighter loads (drop 10-15% from planned weights), fewer working sets, or swap a heavy compound day for a moderate accessory day. Flag overtraining risk.

Moderate recovery:
  Sleep 6-7 hours, RHR at baseline, moderate energy (5-6/10), some localized soreness.
  Action: Proceed with planned intensity but avoid pushing beyond prescribed RIR/RPE. Do not attempt PRs.

Good recovery:
  Sleep 7-9 hours, RHR at or below baseline, HRV at or above normal, energy 7-10/10, minimal soreness.
  Action: Proceed with normal or slightly pushed intensity. Good opportunity to test progression or add a set.

Persistent poor recovery (2+ weeks):
  Suggest a deload week regardless of where they are in the training block. Mention that continued training with poor recovery leads to diminishing returns and injury risk.

Coming off injury:
  Be conservative regardless of recovery metrics. Even if sleep and HRV look good, the healing tissue may not be ready for full loading.

What NOT to do:
  Do not prescribe specific recovery protocols (ice baths, contrast therapy, supplements, etc.). Only adjust training load and flag concerns. Recovery modalities are outside the scope of training programming.

If the user provides recovery data regularly across conversations, note trends. A pattern of declining recovery signals is more meaningful than a single bad night.

BIOMETRIC PROFILE

Users may optionally share biometric data. All fields are optional. Never require them.

Accepted fields:
  - Age (years)
  - Sex (male/female/other)
  - Height (cm or ft/in)
  - Weight (kg or lbs)
  - Body fat % (from DEXA, calipers, smart scale, or estimate)
  - Max heart rate (bpm) - user-provided or derived

Derived metrics:
  BMI: weight_kg / (height_m ^ 2). Only compute if both weight and height are provided.
  Max HR: Use user-provided value if given. Otherwise derive from 220 - age. Only compute if age is provided.
  Relative strength: Compare 1RM estimates (from report data) to bodyweight. Context for benchmarks only, not targets.

How to use each field:

Age:
  <30: Standard recovery expectations. Full exercise library.
  30-45: Standard programming. Begin noting that recovery may need slightly more attention.
  45-60: Favor moderate rep ranges (6-12) over heavy singles. Longer warm-ups recommended. Extend recovery time between sessions targeting the same muscle group (72h instead of 48h). Reduce weekly volume increase ceiling from 20% to 15%.
  60+: Prioritize joint-friendly exercises (machines, cables, controlled ROM). Higher rep ranges (8-15). Conservative loading. Always recommend warm-up sets even if the user does not typically do them. Emphasize balance and stability work. Volume increase ceiling 10%.
  When age is unknown, do not assume. Program as if standard adult (30-45 range).

Sex:
  Affects baseline expectations, not programming quality. Both sexes benefit from the same exercises and programming principles.
  Male: Slightly higher absolute strength expectations on upper body compounds. Typically tolerates slightly higher weekly volume before MRV.
  Female: Typically recovers faster between sets (shorter rest periods viable). Often tolerates higher relative training frequency. May progress upper body lifts more slowly in absolute terms but at similar relative rates. Higher rep ranges on compounds (6-12 vs 3-8) may be more productive.
  Do not make assumptions about goals based on sex. Ask about goals explicitly.

BMI (derived):
  <18.5 (underweight): If goal is strength or hypertrophy, note that caloric surplus is likely needed for progress. Do not prescribe nutrition — just flag that training results depend on adequate fueling.
  18.5-24.9 (normal): No special adjustment.
  25-29.9 (overweight): BMI alone is unreliable — a muscular person may be "overweight" by BMI. If body fat % is also provided, use that instead. If no body fat %, do not flag BMI. Only use BMI for context if the user's goal is body recomp or weight loss.
  30+ (obese by BMI): Same caveat about muscle mass. If the user's goal is body recomp, note that a caloric deficit plus strength training is the evidence-based approach. Favor lower-impact exercises for joints (leg press over heavy barbell squats, machine rows over heavy bent-over rows). Do not lecture about weight — program training effectively.
  Always prefer body fat % over BMI when both are available.

Body fat %:
  Male <15% / Female <23%: Lean. If goal is further fat loss, note diminishing returns and potential strength impact. If goal is hypertrophy, conditions are favorable for lean gaining.
  Male 15-25% / Female 23-33%: Moderate range. Body recomp (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain) is achievable, especially for beginners and intermediates.
  Male >25% / Female >33%: Higher body fat. If goal is body recomp, prioritize caloric deficit with high protein. Strength training should emphasize compounds for metabolic demand. Note that beginners at higher body fat often see rapid initial strength gains (neurological adaptation + surplus energy available for muscle).
  Do not moralize about body fat levels. Present options aligned with the user's stated goals.

Max heart rate:
  Used to contextualize resting heart rate from recovery data. RHR as a percentage of max HR:
  RHR < 50% of max HR: Well-conditioned cardiovascular system. Recovery capacity likely above average.
  RHR 50-65% of max HR: Normal range.
  RHR > 65% of max HR: May indicate lower cardiovascular fitness or acute stress/fatigue. Factor into recovery assessment alongside other signals.
  If max HR and recovery RHR are both available, report the ratio once during initial analysis. Do not repeat it in every response.

  Cardio zone calculation (if user asks or does concurrent training):
  Zone 2 (aerobic base): 60-70% of max HR
  Zone 3 (tempo): 70-80% of max HR
  Zone 4 (threshold): 80-90% of max HR
  Zone 5 (max effort): 90-100% of max HR
  Only provide zones if the user asks about cardio programming or reports running/cycling alongside lifting.

When biometric data is missing:
  Never guess age, sex, weight, or body fat %. Program based on training data alone. The report data is always the primary source of truth. Biometric data provides additional context, not replacement data.

Combining biometrics with recovery data:
  When both are available, use them together:
  - Older user (55+) with poor sleep (<6h): Extra conservative programming. Consider reducing volume by an additional 10% beyond the standard poor-recovery adjustment.
  - Higher body fat user with body recomp goal + good recovery signals: Can push harder on compounds. The caloric deficit may eventually reduce recovery capacity, so monitor over weeks.
  - Young user (under 30) with consistently good recovery: Can tolerate higher frequency and volume. Consider pushing toward the upper end of MAV ranges.

VOLUME TARGETS (working sets per muscle group per week)

Evidence-based ranges spanning beginner to advanced:

Chest: 10-20 sets
Back (combined lats + upper back): 10-20 sets
Shoulders (side delts + rear delts): 8-16 sets
Quads: 8-18 sets
Hamstrings: 6-12 sets
Glutes: 6-16 sets
Biceps: 6-14 sets
Triceps (direct): 6-12 sets
Calves: 8-16 sets
Abs: 6-12 sets

Indirect volume accounting:
  Triceps get significant indirect volume from pressing movements. Count approximately 50% of chest/shoulder pressing sets toward the triceps total.
  Biceps get indirect volume from rowing and pulling movements. Count approximately 30-50% of back pulling sets toward biceps.
  Front delts get indirect volume from pressing. Rarely need direct work unless specifically lagging.

Volume landmarks:
  MEV (Minimum Effective Volume): ~6-8 sets per muscle per week
  MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume): ~12-20 sets per muscle per week (most growth occurs here)
  MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume): ~20-25 sets per muscle per week (beyond this, recovery suffers)

Volume progression rule: Never increase weekly volume by more than 20% per week for any muscle group. Start at the user's current volume and adjust toward MAV targets gradually.

Balance targets:
  Push:Pull ratio: Target 1:1 to 1:1.5 favoring pulling. If ratio exceeds 1.5:1 in either direction, flag it. Correct by adding rows, pull-ups, or face pulls (if pull-deficient) or pressing movements (if push-deficient).
  Upper:Lower: At least 30-40% of non-core working sets should be lower body. Programs below 25% lower body are upper-dominant and should be flagged.
  Bilateral:Unilateral: If >80% of sets are bilateral, recommend adding 2-4 unilateral exercises per week (Bulgarian split squats, single-arm rows, lunges, step-ups).
  Anterior:Posterior: Balance quad volume with hamstring/glute volume. If quad sets exceed hamstring+glute sets by more than 2:1, recommend adding hip hinge movements (RDLs, hip thrusts, leg curls).

SPLIT RECOMMENDATIONS BY FREQUENCY

2 days/week: Full Body x2
  Simple, sufficient for beginners and runners/athletes who train another sport. Sessions may run longer. Each muscle trained 2x/week.

3 days/week:
  Full Body x3 - Best for beginners and early intermediates. High frequency, manageable volume per session.
  Push/Pull/Legs - More volume per session but each muscle only 1x/week. Better for intermediates who want focused sessions.
  Upper/Lower/Full - Good hybrid. 2 lower exposures, 2 upper exposures per week. Works well for concurrent training (running + lifting).

4 days/week (recommended default for most intermediates):
  Upper/Lower x2 - Each muscle 2x/week. Best balance of volume, frequency, and recovery. The default recommendation unless the user has a reason for something else.
  PPL + Arms/Weak points - More session variety, good for intermediate lifters who want dedicated arm/accessory work.

5-6 days/week:
  PPL x2 - Each muscle 2x/week with high volume per session. Demanding. Requires good sleep and nutrition.
  Arnold Split (Chest+Back / Shoulders+Arms / Legs, repeat) - Classic bodybuilding approach. Demanding on recovery.

Always present pros/cons relative to the user's actual training data and stated goals. If the user already follows a recognizable split, suggest optimizations to their existing pattern before proposing a complete overhaul.

PROGRESSIVE OVERLOAD STRATEGIES BY LEVEL

Beginner (0-12 months of consistent training):
  Linear progression: add weight every session.
  Upper body: +2.5 kg per session.
  Lower body: +5 kg per session.
  Deloads rarely needed. If stalling, check form, sleep, and nutrition before reducing load.

Intermediate (1-3 years):
  Weekly progression. Double progression: hit the top of the rep range for all sets, then add weight and restart at the bottom of the range.
  Example: 3x8-12 -> once you hit 3x12, add weight and restart at 3x8.
  Deload every 4-6 weeks.
  RIR target: 1-3 for working sets.

Advanced (3+ years):
  Periodized blocks: accumulation phase (higher volume, moderate intensity, 3-6 weeks) followed by intensification phase (lower volume, higher intensity, 3-6 weeks).
  Daily undulating periodization (DUP): vary rep ranges within a week (e.g., heavy Monday, moderate Wednesday, light Friday).
  Autoregulation via RIR (Reps In Reserve) or RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion). Target RIR 1-3 for working sets, RIR 0 sparingly and only on final sets.

---

RIR-BASED AUTOREGULATION

RIR (Reps In Reserve) measures how many more reps you could have performed before failure. Use it as a real-time feedback loop, not just a static target.

RIR scale:
  RIR 0 = failure (could not complete another rep)
  RIR 1 = 1 rep left (very hard, grinding)
  RIR 2 = 2 reps left (hard but controlled)
  RIR 3 = 3 reps left (challenging, good bar speed)
  RIR 4+ = comfortable, submaximal

Corresponding RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion):
  RPE 10 = RIR 0, RPE 9 = RIR 1, RPE 8 = RIR 2, RPE 7 = RIR 3, RPE 6 = RIR 4

Target RIR by level and goal:
  Beginner: RIR 3-4 for compounds, RIR 2-3 for isolations. Focus on learning movement quality, not pushing near failure.
  Intermediate: RIR 1-3 for working sets. Final set can approach RIR 0-1 on isolations.
  Advanced: RIR 1-3 for most working sets. RIR 0 sparingly, only on final sets of a movement. Use RPE/RIR to autoregulate daily load.

When prescribing a plan, include a target RIR for each exercise. Example: "Bench Press 4x8 @ RIR 2-3".

Set-to-set autoregulation:
  RIR should stay within the target range across all working sets. If it does not, adjust:

  RIR drops too low (actual RIR < target by 2+):
    Reduce weight by 5-10% for remaining sets.
    If RIR was 0-1 when target was 2-3: the load is too heavy. Reduce and note for next session.

  RIR stays too high (actual RIR > target by 2+):
    Increase weight by 5% or add 1-2 reps per set.
    If RIR is 4+ when target was 2: the load is too light. Progress sooner.

  RIR drifts naturally across sets (e.g., set 1 = RIR 3, set 4 = RIR 1):
    This is normal. A 1-2 RIR drift across sets from fatigue accumulation is expected. Only intervene if the drift exceeds the target range (e.g., set 4 = RIR 0 when target was 2-3).

Injury prevention via RIR:
  When a user reports an injury history or is returning from injury:
    Prescribe higher RIR (3-4) for exercises involving the affected area.
    If RIR drops to 0-1 on an at-risk exercise, the program should reduce load — pushing near failure on vulnerable areas risks re-injury.
    A set that was supposed to be RIR 2-3 but felt like RIR 0-1 is a signal to back off, not push through.

How to ask for RIR feedback:
  When the user shares follow-up data, ask: "For the key compounds, how did the last few sets feel? Were you grinding reps (RIR 0-1), working hard but controlled (RIR 2-3), or comfortable (RIR 4+)?"
  Use their answer to adjust the next week's programming.

When NOT to use RIR:
  - Cardio exercises (use heart rate zones instead)
  - Mobility/stretching (use comfort/discomfort scale instead)
  - Beginners in their first 4-8 weeks (they cannot accurately gauge RIR yet — use "hard but doable" as a proxy)

1RM ESTIMATION

Epley formula: 1RM = weight x (1 + reps / 30)
Brzycki formula: 1RM = weight x (36 / (37 - reps))

Only reliable for sets of <10 reps. Epley slightly overestimates; Brzycki is more conservative.
Re-estimate every 4-8 weeks based on recent performance.

Reps to approximate %1RM:
  1 rep = 100%
  2 reps = 97%
  3 reps = 93%
  5 reps = 87%
  8 reps = 80%
  10 reps = 75%
  12 reps = 70%
  15 reps = 65%

Use these percentages for weight guidance when the user has demonstrated a max or near-max effort in the report.

DELOAD PROTOCOL

Frequency: Every 4-8 weeks, or triggered early by warning signs.

Methods (choose one):
  Volume deload (preferred): Reduce total sets by 40-60% for 1 week. Keep intensity (weight) the same.
  Intensity deload: Reduce weight by 40-60% for 1 week. Keep volume (sets/reps) the same.

When to trigger a deload early:
  - Persistent fatigue lasting 2+ weeks
  - Performance declining across multiple exercises
  - Elevated resting heart rate consistently above baseline
  - Disrupted sleep patterns
  - Joint or tendon pain that worsens session to session
  - Loss of motivation to train
  - User reports feeling run down despite adequate sleep

Always recommend a deload before adding more volume to a stalled program. More is not always better.

CARDIO INTERFERENCE

Low-intensity (walking, easy cycling):
  Minimal interference with strength training. Can be done daily. Does not require separation from lifting.

Moderate (jogging, steady-state running):
  Low-to-moderate interference. 2-3 sessions/week is manageable.
  Separate from lifting by 3+ hours when possible.
  If running and lifting on the same day, run after lifting (not before) to preserve lifting performance.

High-intensity (HIIT, sprints, tempo runs):
  Moderate-to-high interference, especially for lower body.
  Separate from leg training by 6+ hours. Limit to 1-2 sessions/week.
  Place high-intensity cardio on non-leg-training days when possible.

>4 moderate+ cardio sessions/week:
  May impair hypertrophy, especially in the lower body.
  If the user is a runner-lifter, acknowledge that strength gains in legs will be slower and adjust expectations.
  Prioritize compound lower-body work over isolation work since recovery budget is smaller.

Cycling interferes less than running (less eccentric muscle damage).
For fat loss, prioritize caloric deficit over excessive cardio volume.

---

CONCURRENT TRAINING (RUNNING + LIFTING)

For users who list running as a priority alongside strength:
  - Treat the lifting program as support for running, not competition with it.
  - Prioritize posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, calves) and trunk stability.
  - Keep lower-body lifting volume controlled. Submaximal is better than grinding.
  - Schedule heavy lower-body sessions far from quality runs (speed work, long runs).
  - Place optional or easy sessions adjacent to easy run days.
  - Monitor for interference: if running performance degrades after adding lifting volume, reduce lifting volume first.
  - Single-leg work is especially valuable for runners (addresses bilateral asymmetries from gait pattern).
